Introduction

It is no question that jobs in the technology industry are a large part of the U.S. job market. In fact, net technology employment accounts for 7.9% of the total U.S. workforce [1]. Furthermore, the growth rate of the technology industry has seen a significant increase since the COVID-19 pandemic. For example, 2019 saw a growth rate of technology jobs of 0.1% while the most recent figure has jumped to 2% [2]. That is a 1900% increase!

When dividing the numerous sectors the technology industry has to offer and analyzing their growth, roles that might be of particular interest to students in the Georgetown's Master's of Data Science program include, but are not limited to, data analysis, data science and machine learning. The Bureau of Labor Statistics predicts 23%, 36% and 21% in employment growth rate for 2021-2031 in those fields respectively. However, what's really the difference between these three technology job types?

There have been many times when family and friends asked the same question, or rather, "what does a data scientist do exactly?" I ask the same question myself sometimes; especially when starting the job search process since many companies post applications for these roles that indeed have very similar descriptions. This project has presented the perfect opportunity to dive deeper into the technology job market by visually analyzing web crawling results for data analysis, data science and machine learning roles posted by companies looking to hire.

Conclusion & Future Exploration

The visual journey on the outlook of the data analysis, data science and machine learning job market has provided valuable insights into current trends and opportunities in these three technological industries. The use of donut charts and treemaps provided clear distinctions in key words that describe the responsibilities and qualifications of these roles; highlighting statistics and machine learning algorithms for data science so thank you to ANLY511 and ANLY501. The usage of sankey diagrams and chloropleth maps helped identify location trends for these roles; while the majority of all job roles are not remote and are relatively split evenly between the coasts and the midwest, it can vary by industry as we saw machine learning roles specifically had more non-remote jobs and data analysis/data science roles were heavily located in the midwest. The network graph provided insight as to the types of companies that have open roles for all three job types while others that may only be looking for a specific one. Finally, the heatmap and boxplot displayed the majority of open roles are full-time only and compensation for those full-time opportunities can vary by each job type, even when comparing results pulled from all over the US to the DC area specifically.

To take this analysis further, there are three steps I would like to take. One challenge encountered through this process was the small sample size of data because of the angle taken to analyze data analysis, data science, and machine learning specifically. Therefore, the first would be to expand the dataset to include at least 100 (ideally 500) query results for each of the three roles isolated in this project. The second step would be to perform further research on ways to add aditional filters to each figure to create even more informative visualizations. For instance, add an additional layer to the network graph so a user may be able to filter by location and observe any data analysis, data science, and machine learning trends by industry that way. The final step involves the creation of a dashboard with similar visualizations that pulls the latest job search data on a timed cadence that can be made public to DSAN students. Although the DSAN program and Georgetown in general have excellent career resources, I believe a current visual dashbaord would be a unique addition to our program.
